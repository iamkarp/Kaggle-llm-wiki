## 1. Corporación Favorita Grocery Sales Forecasting (2017)

**Task:** Time series — forecast unit sales for 4,000+ SKUs across 54 Ecuadorian grocery stores, 16-day horizon.
**Discussion:** https://www.kaggle.com/c/favorita-grocery-sales-forecasting/discussion/47582

**Approach:**
The 1st place solution combined gradient boosted trees and neural networks in a large ensemble (reportedly ~42 models total), with LightGBM forming the backbone. Data was segmented into groups with similar time-series behavior — by store, category, and department — and modeled separately. Final predictions blended non-recursive (direct) and recursive forecasting strategies to balance short- and long-horizon accuracy.

**Key Techniques:**
1. LightGBM with Tweedie regression objective — robust to zero-inflated sales distributions
2. Time-series grouping by store/category/department for segment-specific patterns
3. Rich lag and rolling-window features: price statistics (min, max, std, mean), price momentum by month/year, price normalization
4. Calendar features: day, week, month, year index, day-of-week, weekend flags, holidays, oil price
5. Ensemble of direct and recursive models, model selection via mean/std of OOF cross-validation blended with public LB score

**How to Reuse:**
- Tweedie objective is the right choice whenever the target has a heavy zero mass and right skew (retail, insurance, logistics)
- Always build both recursive and direct multi-step forecasters and blend them — they make complementary errors
- Price momentum and price-relative features (normalized to store-level mean) often matter more than raw price in retail series
- Segment your time series by natural groupings before modeling; a single global model frequently underperforms segment-specific models on heterogeneous retail data

## 2. AI Mathematical Olympiad — Progress Prize 1 (2024)

**Task:** Math reasoning — solve 50 competition-level math problems with an AI system within a 9-hour inference budget on 2×T4 GPUs.
**Discussion:** https://www.kaggle.com/c/ai-mathematical-olympiad-prize/discussion/519303

**Approach:**
Team Numina (Project Numina) fine-tuned DeepSeekMath-7B-Base in two supervised stages: first on ~860k problems with chain-of-thought (CoT) natural-language solutions, then on ~60k problems with tool-integrated reasoning (TIR) paths generated by GPT-4 that interleave rationales, Python code blocks, and execution outputs. At inference they ran a novel self-consistency TIR (SC-TIR) algorithm: sample 48 candidate solution paths per problem with Python execution feedback at each step, then majority-vote the final answers — solving 29/50 problems on the private set.

**Key Techniques:**
1. Two-stage SFT: Stage 1 = CoT fine-tuning on NuminaMath-CoT; Stage 2 = TIR fine-tuning on GPT-4–generated TORA-format traces with execution output
2. SC-TIR decoding: N=48 candidates, M=4 self-correction depth, majority voting over post-processed answers
3. 8-bit AutoGPTQ quantization to fit a 7B model on Kaggle's T4 GPUs without bfloat16 support
4. Full fine-tuning (no LoRA) with DeepSpeed ZeRO-3, gradient checkpointing, 8×H100 training
5. Multi-set internal validation (AMC, AIME, MATH Level 4/5) to avoid public-LB overfitting on a 50-problem test

**How to Reuse:**
- For math-reasoning tasks, TIR (interleaved code execution) outperforms pure CoT; always give the model a Python interpreter
- SC-TIR's self-correction loop — feed execution tracebacks back to the model as context — is broadly applicable to any agent that calls external tools
- Majority voting dramatically reduces variance on small test sets; always combine with diverse sampling (temperature, top-p)
- When constrained to small GPUs, 8-bit quantization can be preferable to bfloat16/float16 degradation — benchmark carefully

## 3. Google Isolated Sign Language Recognition (2023)

**Task:** Sequence classification — recognize 250 isolated ASL signs from MediaPipe landmark sequences (hands, pose, face) in a TFLite model.
**Discussion:** https://www.kaggle.com/c/asl-signs/discussion/406684

**Approach:**
The winning solution adapted a Squeezeformer encoder — a speech-processing architecture — to operate on landmark sequences instead of audio spectrograms. The encoder ingests 130 landmarks (21 per hand, 6 pose points per arm, 76 facial landmarks) across time, treating landmark frames as tokens. A TransformerDecoder (adapted from TFSpeech2Text with LLaMA-style attention and KV-cache support) was attached to produce the class label. The entire pipeline was optimized to run as a TensorFlow Lite model within Kaggle's inference constraints.

**Key Techniques:**
1. Improved Squeezeformer encoder adapted for 2D landmark input rather than 1D audio features
2. 130-landmark subset: both hands (42 kps), 12 arm-pose points, 76 face landmarks (lips, nose, eyes) — carefully curated
3. TransformerDecoder with LLaMA-style attention and KV-cache for autoregressive label generation at inference
4. Augmentation designed specifically for landmark data (coordinate jitter, time-warping, random frame dropout)
5. TFLite compilation with attention-cache support for competitive inference speed under competition constraints

**How to Reuse:**
- Speech/audio architectures (Squeezeformer, Conformer, wav2vec) transfer effectively to any ordered-landmark sequence task; treat landmark frames as "spectrogram frames"
- Curate which keypoints to include — not all MediaPipe outputs help; facial landmarks near the mouth and eyes dominate for sign recognition
- KV-caching in the decoder is essential when deploying autoregressive models under tight latency constraints
- For landmark-based tasks, spatial normalization (centering on dominant hand wrist) and temporal normalization (fixed frame count via interpolation) are critical preprocessing steps

## 4. MAP — Charting Student Math Misunderstandings (2025)

**Task:** NLP/Education — given a student's wrong answer to a math question, retrieve the correct misconception from a 2,500-label taxonomy; evaluated MAP@3.
**Discussion:** https://www.kaggle.com/c/map-charting-student-math-misunderstandings/discussion/612268

**Approach:**
The top solutions framed the problem as a dense retrieval + reranking pipeline. A large language model (Qwen2.5 family) was fine-tuned with QLoRA using contrastive learning on both real and synthetically generated examples to create a strong embedding model. An initial candidate set of misconceptions was retrieved via embedding similarity, then a cross-encoder reranker (or a larger LLM acting as reranker) produced the final top-3 ranked list. Synthetic data generation from the misconception taxonomy was a key differentiator.

**Key Techniques:**
1. Two-stage retrieval: QLoRA-fine-tuned Qwen2.5 embedding model for fast first-stage retrieval, cross-encoder reranker for final ranking
2. Contrastive learning (e.g., InfoNCE / NTXent) on hard negatives sampled from the 2,500-label taxonomy
3. Synthetic data generation: LLM-authored wrong-answer/misconception pairs to augment sparse real labels
4. Scaling trick for unseen misconceptions: generalize from seen label semantics to zero-shot retrieved misconceptions
5. MAP@3 optimization: calibrate retrieval threshold and reranker score to maximize top-3 precision specifically

**How to Reuse:**
- Any multi-class classification problem with 1,000+ classes and sparse labels should be treated as retrieval + reranking, not flat softmax
- Synthetic negative and positive examples from the label taxonomy dramatically improve contrastive learning when real labeled examples are scarce
- A lightweight bi-encoder for first-stage retrieval paired with a heavier cross-encoder reranker is the standard production-ready pattern for this class of problem
- MAP@K optimization: use OOF scores to tune retrieval cut-off K and reranker blend weights specifically against the MAP@K objective

## 5. Google Landmark Recognition 2021

**Task:** Computer vision — recognize 81,313 landmark classes from photographs; fine-grained instance-level recognition at massive scale.
**Discussion:** https://www.kaggle.com/c/landmark-recognition-2021/discussion/277098

**Approach:**
Winner Christof Henkel (Dieter) built an ensemble of DOLG (Deep Orthogonal Fusion of Local and Global Features) models on EfficientNet backbones and hybrid Swin-Transformer/EfficientNet architectures, all trained on the GLDv2 dataset using distributed training on 8×V100 GPUs. DOLG combines global image descriptors with local attention-weighted features through orthogonal projection, enabling both high-level category discrimination and fine-grained instance matching. The ensemble achieved 0.513 private recognition score, published as a peer-reviewed paper.

**Key Techniques:**
1. DOLG architecture: orthogonal projection of local (spatial attention-weighted) and global (pooled) feature vectors for superior discriminability
2. EfficientNet-B5/B6/B7 at 768px with stride-2 modification to capture fine-grained texture
3. Hybrid Swin-Transformer/EfficientNet models at multiple resolutions providing backbone diversity for ensembling
4. ArcFace (additive angular margin) loss for metric learning with 81K+ classes
5. Distributed training across 8×V100 GPUs on full GLDv2 dataset; inference-time retrieval over a gallery index

**How to Reuse:**
- For any fine-grained recognition or retrieval task (>10K classes), use metric learning (ArcFace, CosFace) rather than softmax — it generalizes better to unseen classes at test time
- DOLG's orthogonal local+global fusion is directly applicable to product recognition, vehicle re-ID, and face recognition
- Higher resolution (768px+) with reduced stride provides significant gains on fine-grained tasks; this is often more cost-effective than adding model capacity
- Always build a gallery/index at inference time — classification head accuracy on large-class problems is a ceiling; nearest-neighbor retrieval in embedding space often beats it

## 6. CIBMTR — Equity in Post-HCT Survival Predictions (2024)

**Task:** Medical tabular — predict survival after allogeneic hematopoietic cell transplantation, with the metric penalizing models that are inequitable across racial/ethnic subgroups (stratified concordance index).
**Discussion:** https://www.kaggle.com/c/equity-post-HCT-survival-predictions/discussion/566550

**Approach:**
The 1st place solution (user "minerppdy") framed the problem as predicting two targets simultaneously — a survival risk score and a secondary calibration target — then ensembled both. By optimizing two objectives jointly, the model achieved better calibration across demographic subgroups, which the equity-aware metric directly rewarded. The final ensemble blended multiple survival models (including Cox proportional hazard variants with a custom loss) to maximize the stratified concordance index.

**Key Techniques:**
1. Dual-target modeling: predict both a survival score and a second target (e.g., time-to-event bin) to improve subgroup calibration
2. Cox-loss neural network heads alongside gradient-boosted survival models for ensemble diversity
3. Equity-aware validation: stratify CV folds by race/ethnicity, optimize the stratified concordance metric directly
4. Feature engineering from clinical variables: HLA mismatch degree, donor type, conditioning intensity, disease status at transplant
5. Ensemble blending of survival model families (Cox PH, random survival forest, GBM with survival objective) for robust rankings

**How to Reuse:**
- When a competition uses a fairness-penalized metric, include subgroup membership as a direct feature and stratify your cross-validation by the protected attribute
- Dual-target training (primary rank + secondary calibration) is a general technique to improve concordance without sacrificing discrimination
- Cox-loss GBMs (LightGBM/XGBoost with survival objectives) are strong baselines for any time-to-event task; add neural Cox heads for ensemble diversity
- Recalculate HLA matching from raw allele data rather than relying on processed fields — data quality issues in clinical datasets are common and impactful

## 7. Jigsaw Multilingual Toxic Comment Classification (2020)

**Task:** NLP — binary toxicity detection for comments in 6 test languages (Spanish, French, Italian, Portuguese, Russian, Turkish) with English-only training labels; cross-lingual transfer.
**Discussion:** https://www.kaggle.com/c/jigsaw-multilingual-toxic-comment-classification/discussion/160862

**Approach:**
Winner (leecming82) used an iterative pseudo-labeling strategy: bootstrap test-set labels using XLM-RoBERTa-Large, then iteratively refine by combining pseudo-labels with training data, fine-tuning monolingual BERT variants for each target language, blending new predictions (weight 0.2–0.8) back into the pseudo-label pool, and repeating across multiple model types. This replaced the standard cross-lingual transfer paradigm with a self-training loop that progressively adapts to each target language.

**Key Techniques:**
1. Iterative pseudo-labeling: XLM-RoBERTa bootstrap → monolingual fine-tuning → prediction blending → repeat
2. Language-specific BERT variants: CamemBERT (French), BETO (Spanish), Italian/Turkish/Portuguese/Russian BERT models
3. Ensemble blending with dynamic weighting (0.2–0.8) calibrated per language-model combination
4. Mixed precision training + gradient accumulation to fit large models (XLM-RoBERTa-Large) on RTX Titan hardware
5. Monolingual FastText BiGRU for non-contextual diversity in the ensemble

**How to Reuse:**
- When labeled data exists only in a source language, iterative pseudo-labeling on target-language test data consistently outperforms zero-shot cross-lingual transfer alone
- Always include monolingual models (even lightweight ones) alongside multilingual transformers — they capture language-specific distributional patterns that multilingual models miss
- Blending pseudo-labels gradually (start at 0.2 weight) prevents early noise from propagating through the entire training loop
- This iterative self-training pattern is directly applicable to any domain-adaptation problem where unlabeled target data is available at competition time

## 8. BNP Paribas Cardif Claims Management (2016)

**Task:** Tabular — binary classification to predict whether an insurance claim should be accelerated (approved without delay); ~130 anonymized features, ~114K rows.
**Discussion:** https://www.kaggle.com/c/bnp-paribas-cardif-claims-management/discussion/20247

**Approach:**
Team "Dexters Lab" (Darius, Davut, Song) won with a deep ensemble of gradient boosted trees, neural networks, and other classifiers. The solution required extensive feature engineering on the anonymized variables, including frequency encoding, pairwise interactions, and missing-value pattern features. XGBoost formed the primary base learner, with neural networks and Extra Trees added for diversity; predictions were blended via a second-level meta-learner (stacking).

**Key Techniques:**
1. XGBoost as primary base model with extensive hyperparameter search
2. Neural network classifiers (multi-layer perceptrons) fine-tuned on the same feature set for ensemble diversity
3. Frequency encoding of categorical columns and pairwise feature interactions among the anonymized variables
4. Missing-value pattern features: binarized NaN indicators and row-level missingness count
5. Two-level stacking: base model OOF predictions as meta-features for a linear/GBM meta-learner

**How to Reuse:**
- On anonymized tabular data, frequency encoding + missingness patterns routinely provide the largest feature-engineering gains when semantic meaning is unknown
- Always add neural network classifiers to a GBM ensemble — even a simple 3-layer MLP trained on the same features reduces GBM correlation and improves blended log-loss
- Two-level stacking (OOF-based) is the standard Kaggle recipe for tabular competitions; use 5-fold or 10-fold to minimize variance in meta-features
- Log-loss minimization on imbalanced binary classification benefits from probability calibration (Platt scaling or isotonic regression) before stacking

## 9. NFL 1st and Future — Impact Detection (2020)

**Task:** Computer vision/video — detect helmet-to-helmet and helmet-to-ground impacts in NFL game video (sideline + endzone), outputting bounding boxes per impact event.
**Discussion:** https://www.kaggle.com/c/nfl-impact-detection/discussion/209403

**Approach:**
The winning pipeline combined YOLOv5 for single-class helmet detection across all frames, optical-flow-based helmet tracking to estimate per-helmet velocity, and a Temporal Shift Module (TSM) classification ensemble to distinguish true impacts (acceleration events) from normal motion (constant velocity). Helmet crops were normalized to 128×128×3×16 spatiotemporal volumes with optical-flow motion correction. Temporal NMS filtered predictions across frames using tracking identity.

**Key Techniques:**
1. YOLOv5 single-class helmet detector — optimized for small-object detection across compressed video frames
2. Optical flow velocity estimation: track each helmet's average velocity over surrounding frames; impacts appear as velocity discontinuities
3. TSM (Temporal Shift Module) on EfficientNet-B0/B3/B5 backbones — joint spatial-temporal modeling at no extra parameter cost
4. Spatiotemporal volume normalization: 128×128px crops × 16 temporal frames, motion-corrected via optical flow
5. Temporal NMS across tracked helmet trajectories; positive label dilation (mark ±3 frames around ground-truth impacts) to handle label timing uncertainty

**How to Reuse:**
- Optical flow velocity is a powerful, lightweight signal for distinguishing "event" frames from "background" frames in any sports/action detection task
- TSM is the most parameter-efficient way to add temporal reasoning to 2D CNN backbones — prefer it over 3D convolutions when GPU memory is limited
- Label dilation (expanding positive frames) is essential in video competitions with imprecise temporal annotations — match dilation width to your validation metric's tolerance window
- Two-view fusion (sideline + endzone) requires careful temporal alignment; use player tracking data when available to synchronize views

## 10. Enefit — Predict Energy Behavior of Prosumers (2023)

**Task:** Time series — predict hourly electricity consumption and solar production for Estonian energy prosumers up to 48 hours ahead; metric MAE on both production and consumption.
**Discussion:** https://www.kaggle.com/c/predict-energy-behavior-of-prosumers/discussion/472793

**Approach:**
The 1st place solution (user "hyd") used XGBoost as the primary model with comprehensive feature engineering: lag features from historical consumption/production, weather forecast features (temperature, solar irradiance, cloud cover, wind speed), installed photovoltaic capacity, and energy price signals. Separate models were trained for consumption and production targets, respecting the physical constraint that production is near-zero at night. Rich calendar and time-of-day features captured seasonality.

**Key Techniques:**
1. XGBoost with separate models per target (consumption vs. production) — treating them as independent forecasting problems
2. Lag and rolling-window features: 24h, 48h, 168h (weekly) lags for both targets, capturing daily and weekly seasonality
3. Weather forecast features from multiple sources: temperature, solar radiation, cloud cover, precipitation, wind speed aligned to prosumer location
4. Installed PV capacity as a multiplicative feature for production models — normalizes production by system size
5. Energy price and imbalance price features as market signals correlated with prosumer behavior

**How to Reuse:**
- For prosumer/distributed energy forecasting, always model production and consumption separately — they have different physical drivers (weather vs. behavioral)
- Installed capacity normalization (production per kWp) is essential before aggregating across prosumers with different system sizes
- Multi-source weather feature fusion (at least 2 forecast providers) consistently improves solar forecasting — forecast errors are partially uncorrelated across providers
- 168h (weekly) lags almost always belong in any energy or retail demand model; business cycles dominate over shorter autocorrelation structure

## 11. RSNA Screening Mammography Breast Cancer Detection (2022)

**Task:** Medical imaging — binary classification of screening mammograms for breast cancer; evaluated by probability F1 (pF1) which heavily penalizes missed cancers.
**Discussion:** https://www.kaggle.com/c/rsna-breast-cancer-detection/discussion/392449

**Approach:**
Winner (dangnh0611) built a two-stage pipeline: a YOLOX-nano detector trained on 521 manually annotated images to crop the breast ROI from each DICOM, followed by 4 ConvNeXt-Small classifiers trained with 4-fold cross-validation on the ROI-cropped images. External datasets (BMCD, CMMD, CDD-CESM, MiniDDSM, VinDr-Mammo) were standardized and incorporated to expand the positive-cancer training set. Final models were converted to TensorRT engines on A100 GPUs for inference.

**Key Techniques:**
1. YOLOX-nano ROI detector: removes background and normalizes breast position — dramatically improves classification signal
2. ConvNeXt-Small classification backbone with 4-fold cross-validation; 8-bit PNG normalization post-crop
3. Multi-source external data integration: 5 external mammography datasets standardized to the same 8-bit PNG format and cancer-label convention
4. TensorRT conversion (Torch → TRT): 2–4× inference speedup on A100, enabling larger image resolution within time limits
5. pF1-optimized threshold selection: calibrate classification cutoff on OOF predictions to maximize the competition-specific metric rather than AUC

**How to Reuse:**
- Always add an ROI detection stage in medical imaging competitions — background clutter is one of the largest sources of noise in whole-image classifiers
- External datasets with different acquisition protocols require careful standardization (bit-depth, VOI LUT, DICOM windowing) before integration; failure to normalize can hurt more than help
- TensorRT conversion should be a default step in any time-constrained medical imaging submission — it often unlocks 2–4× resolution increase within the same wall-clock budget
- pF1 and similar F-beta metrics require per-threshold optimization, not just AUC — tune the decision threshold on your OOF predictions against the competition metric

## 12. U.S. Patent Phrase to Phrase Matching (2022)

**Task:** NLP — score semantic similarity between patent phrase pairs in a specific CPC classification context; evaluated by Pearson correlation coefficient between predicted and human-rated scores.
**Discussion:** https://www.kaggle.com/c/us-patent-phrase-to-phrase-matching/discussion/332243

**Approach:**
Top solutions used DeBERTa-v3 (base and large) fine-tuned directly on the phrase-pair + CPC-context input formatted as a single sequence, treating the task as both regression and ordinal classification. The CPC section code (A–H) was prepended as a special token, making the model domain-aware. Ensembles of DeBERTa variants fine-tuned with different regression vs. classification objectives, pooling strategies, and seeds formed the final submission, with the Pearson metric guiding calibration.

**Key Techniques:**
1. DeBERTa-v3-Large as backbone — superior to RoBERTa and BERT on this kind of NLI/similarity task due to its disentangled attention mechanism
2. Domain-aware formatting: concatenate CPC section description or code with anchor+target phrase as a single input — context dramatically improves similarity scoring
3. Multi-objective training: regression (MSE) + ordinal classification (five score levels) combined in a single loss for calibrated probability outputs
4. Mean-pooling of last N hidden states vs. CLS token — ensembling both pooling strategies adds diversity
5. Pseudo-labeling of patent corpora: continued pre-training (domain-adaptive pretraining) on patent text before fine-tuning improves domain vocabulary alignment

**How to Reuse:**
- Always include domain/category context as part of the input for semantic similarity tasks — the same phrase pair can have very different similarity scores across domains
- DeBERTa-v3 is the default choice for most NLP regression/classification tasks; start here before trying larger models
- Multi-objective training (regression + ordinal classification heads jointly) is a consistent technique for similarity tasks with 4–5 discrete score levels
- Pearson-optimized ensembling: weight individual models proportionally to their OOF Pearson score rather than using uniform averaging

## 13. ALASKA2 Image Steganalysis (2020)

**Task:** Computer vision — detect whether a JPEG image contains a hidden message embedded via one of three steganography algorithms (JMiPOD, JUNIWARD, UERD); 4-class detection, evaluated by weighted AUC.
**Discussion:** https://www.kaggle.com/c/alaska2-image-steganalysis/discussion/168548

**Approach:**
Winner Guanshuo Xu (a steganalysis PhD specialist) leveraged domain expertise from academic steganalysis research. The key insight was applying the "OneHotConv" trick from the steganography literature: a fixed convolutional layer of SRM (Spatial Rich Model) high-pass filters preprocessing the image before a standard CNN backbone (EfficientNet-B2 and variants), forcing the network to operate in the residual domain where steganographic signal is strongest. A large ensemble of models with diverse preprocessing and architectures provided the final margin.

**Key Techniques:**
1. SRM (Spatial Rich Model) filter layer: fixed 30 high-pass convolutional filters applied before the trainable backbone — extracts residuals where steganographic payload concentrates
2. OneHotConv initialization: SRM weights initialized from the SPL paper's filter bank, then partially fine-tuned to adapt to JPEG-specific artifacts
3. EfficientNet-B2/B3 backbones trained on residual-domain features rather than raw pixel values
4. JPEG-aware data augmentation: only augmentations that preserve JPEG block structure (no crop/resize that would destroy 8×8 DCT alignment)
5. Weighted AUC optimization: calibrate ensemble weights across 4 classes to maximize weighted AUC rather than accuracy

**How to Reuse:**
- Domain-specific fixed filter banks (SRM for steganography, Gabor for texture, Laplacian for sharpness) as frozen first layers outperform learned filters when the relevant signal is known to live in a specific residual space
- For JPEG forensics tasks, always operate in the DCT domain or residual domain — raw pixel input discards most forensic signal
- When the evaluation metric is weighted AUC across classes, tune class weights in the loss function to match the competition weights
- Academic steganalysis literature (SRM, DCTR, XuNet) is directly applicable to Kaggle forensics competitions — domain expertise from the research community creates durable moats

## 14. Tweet Sentiment Extraction (2020)

**Task:** NLP — extract the minimal text span from a tweet that "best supports" the tweet's labeled sentiment (positive/negative/neutral); evaluated by Jaccard similarity between predicted and ground-truth spans.
**Discussion:** https://www.kaggle.com/c/tweet-sentiment-extraction/discussion/159477

**Approach:**
Team "Dark of the Moon" built a two-level stacking pipeline. Level 1 used multiple transformer models (RoBERTa-base/large, DistilRoBERTa, XLNet, BERT, BERTweet) fine-tuned as QA extractive span models, each predicting start/end token probabilities. Level 2 converted those probability distributions to character-level features and trained three diverse character-level neural networks (RNN, CNN, WaveNet-inspired) as meta-models. A custom Jaccard-smoothed loss function at Level 1 provided diversity over standard cross-entropy, and pseudo-labeling on public test data added further signal.

**Key Techniques:**
1. QA-style span extraction framing: sentiment label as the "question," tweet text as the "context" — enables use of SQuAD-pretrained weights
2. Multi-model Level 1: RoBERTa-large-squad2, BERT-large-wwm, BERTweet, XLNet with concatenated last-N-layer pooling and multi-sample dropout
3. Custom Jaccard-smoothing loss: computes token-level Jaccard between predicted and ground-truth spans, then optimizes KL divergence — directly aligns training objective with evaluation metric
4. Character-level Level 2 meta-models (RNN + CNN + WaveNet): trained on token probability distributions from Level 1, adding cross-architecture diversity
5. Pseudo-labeling on public test set (confidence threshold 0.35): +0.001–0.002 CV improvement; "leakless" OOF stacking to prevent meta-learner data leakage

**How to Reuse:**
- Span extraction (NER, answer extraction, highlight selection) should always be framed as QA — it enables transfer from massive SQuAD-pretrained models
- Multi-sample dropout during fine-tuning adds regularization and acts as implicit ensembling within a single model; use 5–8 heads with different dropout masks, average their predictions
- Custom metric-aligned losses (here: Jaccard-smoothed cross-entropy) consistently outperform generic cross-entropy when the evaluation metric is non-differentiable — approximate it in the loss
- Character-level meta-models receiving token-level probability features as input is a surprisingly effective stacking pattern for sequence labeling tasks

## 15. Home Credit — Credit Risk Model Stability (2024)

**Task:** Tabular — predict probability of loan default, but evaluated on a stability-adjusted Gini coefficient that penalizes models whose performance degrades across time-ordered weeks of test data.
**Discussion:** https://www.kaggle.com/c/home-credit-credit-risk-model-stability/discussion/508337

**Approach:**
The 1st place solution (user "yuuniee," titled "My Betting Strategy") recognized that the stability penalty — computed as the mean weekly Gini minus the standard deviation of weekly Ginis — incentivizes consistent ranking across time rather than raw discrimination. The winning strategy deliberately engineered temporally stable features (avoiding features that shift distribution over time) and used LightGBM trained with careful cross-validation stratified by week. Aggressive feature selection removed features with high week-to-week variance, trading some raw AUC for dramatically reduced Gini volatility.

**Key Techniques:**
1. Stability-aware feature selection: compute per-feature week-to-week Gini variance; discard features with high temporal instability even if they improve overall AUC
2. Week-stratified cross-validation: fold assignment by time week ensures CV stability metric matches the hidden test evaluation structure
3. LightGBM with monotonicity constraints on key credit bureau features to prevent overfitting to time-specific noise
4. Feature engineering from multiple data sources: bureau aggregates, previous application behavior, installment payment history — all aggregated at varying time windows (3m, 6m, 12m, 24m)
5. Ensemble blend calibrated to minimize Gini standard deviation across folds, not just maximize mean Gini

**How to Reuse:**
- Any competition with a stability-penalized metric requires a new CV design — stratify by the temporal dimension and optimize the composite metric (mean - decay × std) directly
- Deliberately pruning features that introduce temporal distribution shift is counterintuitive but critical: a model that degrades predictably across time often beats one that was overfit to the early period
- Monotonicity constraints in GBMs are powerful regularizers for credit/financial models where domain knowledge dictates monotonic relationships (e.g., higher debt-to-income → higher default risk)
- LightGBM's `feature_importance` with `importance_type='gain'` stratified by time partition is a direct tool for identifying temporally unstable features

## Cross-Cutting Patterns

| Pattern | Competitions | Notes |
|---------|-------------|-------|
| **Ensembling is universal** | 1, 5, 7, 8, 9, 11, 13, 14 | No single-model win in tabular/CV; even NLP uses model averaging |
| **Two-stage pipelines (detect then classify)** | 9 (YOLOv5→TSM), 11 (YOLOX→ConvNeXt) | ROI extraction or object detection as Stage 1 consistently wins in medical/sports CV |
| **QA framing for span tasks** | 14 | Extractive span tasks always benefit from QA-pretrained weights (SQuAD) |
| **Pseudo-labeling / self-training** | 7, 14 | Effective when labeled target-domain data is scarce; requires confidence thresholding |
| **Domain-specific first-layer features** | 13 (SRM filters) | Fixed domain-expert feature extractors outperform learned filters when the signal location is known |
| **Metric-aligned custom losses** | 14 (Jaccard-smooth), 6 (stratified concordance) | Approximate non-differentiable metrics in the training loss for consistent gains |
| **Time-series feature engineering dominates** | 1, 10, 15 | Lags, rolling windows, and calendar features outperform model complexity for structured time-series |
