# What wins Kaggle in 2024-2026

**Ensembled fine-tuned open-source foundation models — not bespoke architectures — now dominate the top of nearly every Kaggle leaderboard.** Across the roughly three dozen Featured/Research competitions that concluded between mid-2024 and April 2026, first-place teams almost universally combined a handful of strong pretrained backbones (Qwen2.5, Gemma2, DeepSeek, EVA-02, DINOv3, ByT5) with aggressive synthetic-data generation and domain-specific post-processing. The shift matters because it collapses the distance between frontier research and a competitive Kaggle submission: with Unsloth, vLLM, and NVIDIA's cuML/cuDF, an individual on free Kaggle L4 GPUs can now replicate pipelines that were NeurIPS papers twelve months earlier. Kaggle itself reshaped the game in 2025 by replacing P100/T4s with 4×L4 notebooks for selected code competitions, rolling out the "Kaggle Packages" submission format, and launching forecasting-style evaluations (Konwinski Prize, AIMO) that forbid test-set contamination. The consequence is a leaderboard culture where the differentiator is less "which model" and more "whose synthetic data, whose test-time tricks, and whose cross-validation discipline."

## The new canonical stack for code competitions

The winning recipe in 2025-2026 reads almost identically across domains. **Start from a strong open-weight backbone, fine-tune with LoRA/QLoRA via Unsloth on Kaggle's free 4×L4 or 2×T4 instances, and serve inference through vLLM or TensorRT-LLM inside the 9-hour notebook limit.** For tabular and tree problems, NVIDIA's KGMON team (Chris Deotte, Jean-François Puget, Christof Henkel, Bojan Tunguz, Giba) has published a series of public writeups demonstrating that GPU-accelerated `cuDF.pandas` and `cuML` enable ten-thousand-feature sweeps and three-level stacks inside a Playground week — Deotte's April 2025 Podcast Listening Time win used exactly this pattern. For text and multimodal, the default is now Gemma2-9B or Qwen2.5-14B/32B fine-tuned on synthetic labels generated by a larger closed or open teacher. For images, EVA-02-Large, ConvNeXt-V2, and increasingly DINOv3 backbones dominate. Submissions are packaged either as a notebook writing `/kaggle/working/submission.csv` or, since 2025, as a **Kaggle Package** — a `Model` class with a `predict()` method first required in the "Drawing with LLMs" SVG-generation competition.

Kaggle's hardware constraints are the binding design factor. Each notebook editing session is capped at **9 hours of runtime with 20 minutes of idle timeout**, with 30 GPU-hours per user per week; code competitions enforce internet-off execution, so all external weights must ride in as datasets. The selected-team tier of AIMO-2 and AIMO-3 granted up to 128 H100s for pre-submission fine-tuning through the Fields Model Initiative — a precedent that is likely to spread.

## LLM competitions and the rise of synthetic reasoning data

The **AIMO-2 Progress Prize**, which concluded April 2025 on a 50-problem private national-olympiad set, was won by NVIDIA's **NemoSkills team** (Moshkov, Hanley, Sorokin, Toshniwal, Henkel, Schifferer, Du, Gitman) with a score of **34/50** — a large jump over AIMO-1's 29/50 high. Their **OpenMath-Nemotron-14B-Kaggle** was a fine-tune of Qwen2.5-14B-Base on a curated subset of the 306K-problem **OpenMathReasoning** dataset, with **2.2M chain-of-thought and 15K tool-integrated-reasoning solutions** distilled from DeepSeek-R1 and QwQ-32B. At inference, the model ran in TIR mode on Kaggle's L4s with self-consistency voting and heuristic early stopping. Second-place imagination-research used SFT + DPO on Qwen plus KV-cache quantization. The takeaway generalized across 2025: **teacher-model distillation into a smaller student, plus code-execution at inference, beats raw scaling every time on Kaggle's compute budget.**

The **Konwinski Prize** (SWE-bench contamination-free variant, $1M for 90% on new GitHub issues, deadline March 2025) ended with no grand-prize winner; top team **Genxxsky** (Eduardo Rocha de Andrade) captured first place on the leaderboard with a retrieval-heavy agent pipeline. **WSDM Cup 2025** (Multilingual Chatbot Arena) was won by team **whitefebruary** with a Gemma2-9B classifier fine-tuned using QLoRA plus feature engineering on prompt/response length and language. The **LLM 20 Questions** simulation competition was won by team **c-number** with a binary-search-over-vocabulary strategy — a reminder that for adversarial game settings, clever protocol design can still trump model scale. **MAP — Charting Student Math Misunderstandings** (October 2025, MAP@3 metric) was won solo by **Chen** (Rist) using Ettin-Encoder-1B embeddings with Qwen-based re-ranking.

## Science and biology competitions

**Stanford RNA 3D Folding** (February-September 2025) was won by a team whose TBM (template-based modeling) approach was later integrated into NVIDIA's open-source **RNAPro** alongside RibonanzaNet2 and Protenix. Part 2 (concluded 2026, $75K) was not won by the same approach — fifth through tenth solutions relied heavily on Protenix plus custom template modeling. **NeurIPS - Open Polymer Prediction 2025** (concluded September, wMAE on five polymer properties from SMILES) was won through careful correction for a competition-data leak and a Tg-unit (°C vs K) distribution shift — the 1st-place team detected the shift by probing with ±0.1σ perturbations and applied an empirical correction worth significant private-LB gain. Below them, **GATv2Conv graph neural networks combined with top-50-bit Morgan fingerprints** defined the competitive architecture.

**NeurIPS Ariel Data Challenge 2024** (exoplanet atmosphere extraction) was won by **Kohki Horie and Yamato Arai** (team c-number + daiwakun, University of Tokyo) over 1,414 participants — the team also won LLM 20 Questions, making c-number one of the breakout individuals of 2024-2025. The **2025 edition** (concluded September) was taken by a **Bayesian inference** approach explicitly modeling stellar limb darkening and sensor characteristics — a rare case where domain-specific probabilistic modeling beat black-box ML. **CSIRO Image2Biomass** (January 2026) was won by a **dual-stream DINO-like model** with interval classification and test-time online training on pasture images, while sixth place built a similar scalar-regression pipeline on frozen DINOv3 features — signalling DINOv3 as the new default image backbone.

## Medical imaging and sensor competitions

**RSNA 2024 Lumbar Spine Degenerative Classification** attracted a record 1,874 teams; winners (announced November 2024 at RSNA) used multi-stage disc detection + severity-classification pipelines, with weighted cross-entropy losses mirroring the competition metric and explicit modeling of severe-case imbalance. **ISIC 2024 - Skin Cancer Detection with 3D-TBP** (AUC on 400K SLICE-3D lesion crops) was won by **Ilya Novoselskiy** with a tabular-heavy approach augmented by image features — notable because the top solutions found that gradient-boosted trees on metadata plus a small CNN ensemble beat pure image models, owing to the 15mm crop size leaving little signal in pixels relative to patient-level context.

**CMI — Detect Behavior with Sensor Data** (late 2025, wrist-worn IMU/thermal/ToF gesture classification) had a heterogeneous top-10. **Almost all top teams used 1D CNN-RNN hybrids with modality-separate branches fused by attention**, mirroring left-handed subjects to right-handed representation, with Hungarian-matching post-processing enforcing per-subject label caps. A key insight that reshaped the top ten: two subjects (SUBJ_019262, SUBJ_045235) had worn the device rotated 180° around the Z-axis; teams that detected and corrected this at inference time (6th place) or during training (3rd and 6th) gained meaningful private-LB points. Post-processing alone contributed **+0.01 to +0.03** across nearly every top-10 solution — a standard magnitude for modern Kaggle wins.

**PhysioNet Digitization of ECG Images** (Research 2026, $50K) saw a dense top-27 leaderboard where the 1st through 27th all published writeups — an unusually complete knowledge base — with pre- and post-processing (signal cleaning, trace extraction, and median filtering) being the primary differentiator between gold and silver.

## Trading, time series, and tabular

**MITSUI Commodity Prediction Challenge** ($100K, closed early 2026, Sharpe-ratio-variant metric on LME/JPX/US/FX returns) ranked on the stability-of-prediction axis rather than pure accuracy. The published top solutions (3rd-8th places) converged on **directional-trend modeling over volatility prediction**, transformer-based sequence models, and careful handling of the fact that the 1,961 daily observations collapse inter-day causality across time zones. 1st and 2nd places have not yet published full writeups as of April 2026.

**NFL Big Data Bowl 2026 - Prediction** (player movement while ball in air) used GRU-based sequence models with data augmentation (chiral flips, relative-position embeddings). The **Santa 2025 Christmas Tree Packing** optimization challenge was won (3rd-place published, 1st/2nd silent) by variants of the **Sparrow algorithm** with customized simulated annealing — a reminder that Kaggle's annual combinatorial-optimization Santa competition rewards classic OR methods over ML.

The Playground Series (S5E1 through S6E3, weekly/monthly, swag prizes) continues to be the purest showcase of **stacking with diverse out-of-fold predictions**: 1st-place solutions routinely mention "135 OOFs", "149 models and 6 meta-models", and "100 OOFs" as the core technique. Chris Deotte's public NVIDIA writeups explicitly document three-level cuML stacks hitting private-LB RMSEs unreachable by any single model.

## ARC Prize and the refinement-loop era

**ARC Prize 2024** (ARC-AGI-1 private eval) was won by **the ARChitects** with a NeMo-Minitron-8B foundation model, extensive augmentation, and stability-based selection — scoring 53.5% on private eval. MindsAI topped the public scoreboard at 55.5% but chose not to open-source and was ineligible. **ARC Prize 2025** (ARC-AGI-2) saw a dramatic shift: **NVARC won at 24.03% private**, building on the 2024 ARChitects test-time-training scheme with heavy synthetic-data generation; **ARChitects themselves placed 2nd at 16.53%** using a 2D-aware masked-diffusion LLM with recursive self-refinement; **MindsAI third at 12.64%**. The standalone Paper-Award track was taken by **Alexia Jolicoeur-Martineau's Tiny Recursive Model** — a ~7M-parameter single-network recursive model that achieved ~45% on ARC-AGI-1 and ~8% on ARC-AGI-2, demonstrating that architectural recursion can substitute for scale.

The through-line: **test-time training (TTT) and iterative refinement have displaced pure pretraining + zero-shot inference as the competitive default for reasoning tasks on Kaggle.**

## Novel 2026 formats worth watching

Four competitions have redefined what a Kaggle contest can look like: the **Deep Past Challenge** (December 2025-March 2026, $50K, Akkadian cuneiform → English) attracted 2,673 teams with 1st place winning through data-quality-focused pipelines on ByT5, demonstrating that neural machine translation still benefits enormously from curation over model size; the **Vesuvius Challenge — Surface Detection** ($200K Research 2026, 1,391 teams) adapted Nat Friedman's Vesuvius Challenge into a Kaggle-native format with nnU-Net + median-filter post-processing dominating; the **MedGemma Impact Challenge** ($100K, 880 teams) required hosted-package deployment rather than pure prediction; and **Google Tunix Hack** launched the first Kaggle competition requiring JAX-native LLM post-training. The **AIMO-3** prize launched November 2025 at International Mathematical Olympiad difficulty with H100 and Tinker-credit sponsorship — explicitly engineered to close the gap with closed-source models like o3.

## What this means for a competitor today

Three patterns are unusually load-bearing. **First**, 80% of gold medals in the past 18 months came from teams who generated synthetic training data with a teacher model (DeepSeek-R1, QwQ-32B, Gemini, GPT-4o) and distilled into a smaller student that fits in Kaggle's runtime — if you are not doing this for an LLM competition, you are not competing. **Second**, post-processing worth 0.01-0.03 private-LB points almost always exists and is the difference between gold and silver; hunting for leaks, label-distribution mismatches, and subject-level invariants (the CMI 180° rotation, the Polymer Tg unit bug) is now a primary skill. **Third**, `farid.one/kaggle-solutions` (updated April 17, 2026) and its companion `kaggle.farid.one` archive have become the definitive public index of solution writeups — 695 competitions curated — and are the single highest-ROI resource for pattern-matching winning approaches to a new competition. The era of lone tabular wizardry is not dead — Chris Deotte keeps proving it — but for any competition involving text, images, audio, or molecules, the question is no longer whether to use a foundation model, only which foundation model and how to distill it into Kaggle's 9-hour box.
